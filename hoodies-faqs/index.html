Hoodies questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A hoodie is a casual, midweight top with a hood, and your main choice is between a zip-up (easy layering, adjustable) and a pullover (warmer, no zipper to snag). Fabric weight and fit matter more than brand names. Look for cotton-poly blends for durability, French terry or fleece for warmth, and a fit that allows a thin layer underneath without being baggy.

What’s the difference between a zip-up hoodie and a pullover hoodie?

A zip-up hoodie has a full-length front zipper, so you can open it completely for ventilation or layering over a tee or shirt. A pullover hoodie (sometimes called a crewneck hoodie or just “hoodie”) slips over your head and usually has a kangaroo pocket. Zip-ups are more versatile for transitional weather, you can wear them unzipped like a jacket or zipped up as a midlayer. Pullovers tend to trap heat better because there’s no zipper gap, and they often feel more relaxed and classic. If you run warm or plan to wear the hoodie mostly open, go zip-up. If you want a cozy, no-fuss piece for lounging or cold days, pullover is the traditional choice.

What fabric weight should I look for in a hoodie?

Hoodie fabric weight is usually described as lightweight, midweight, or heavyweight. Lightweight hoodies (around “French terry” or thin jersey) are good for layering under a jacket or wearing in mild weather. Midweight hoodies are the most common, thick enough to feel substantial, but not too hot for indoor wear. Heavyweight hoodies (often called “brushed fleece” or “12 oz.” in the trade) are stiff, warm, and hold their shape. For year-round use, a midweight cotton-poly fleece is a safe bet. If you run cold or want a hoodie that works as a light jacket, pick a heavyweight option. Lightweight hoodies are best for the gym or summer evenings. Avoid anything that feels papery or super thin unless you intend to wear it strictly as a base layer.

How should a hoodie fit?

Fit preference varies, but a good rule is that the shoulder seam should sit at the edge of your shoulder, not drooping down your arm. The torso should be close enough that there’s no excess fabric bunching at the lower back, but roomy enough to lift your arms without the hem riding up to your waist. Sleeves should end at your wrist bone. If you plan to layer a hoodie over a thick flannel or under a coat, size up. If you want a trim, athletic look, stick with your usual size. Be aware that many hoodies shrink in length and sleeve width after washing, especially 100% cotton ones; if you prefer a longer torso, consider a “tall” size or a cotton-poly blend that resists shrinkage.

What are the best hoodie materials for different seasons?

For cold weather, look for “fleece-lined” or “brushed” interiors, these trap air for warmth. Wool-blend hoodies are also excellent for winter but require more careful washing. For fall and spring, a cotton-poly French terry is breathable and comfortable. In summer, a lightweight cotton jersey hoodie (or even a mesh athletic hoodie) provides sun protection and can be layered over a tank top. Avoid 100% polyester hoodies if you want natural breathability, they don’t let moisture escape well. Blends with spandex or elastane add stretch but can make the fabric feel slippery. For durability, a midweight cotton-poly blend (around 60/40 or 50/50) is hard to beat and softens with age.

How do I care for my hoodie to make it last?

Most hoodies hold up best when washed inside out in cold water on a gentle cycle. Hot water and high heat drying are the main enemies, they cause fading, shrinking, and pilling. If you must tumble dry, use low heat and remove the hoodie while it’s still slightly damp, then lay it flat to finish drying. Avoid fabric softeners; they coat the fibers and reduce the hoodie’s ability to wick moisture (if it’s a performance blend) and can make fleece feel waxy. For pilling, use a fabric shaver or a sweater stone. Store hoodies folded, not on thin hangers, which can stretch the shoulders out of shape.

Frequently asked questions

Are pullover hoodies warmer than zip-up hoodies?

Generally, yes. A pullover has no zipper, so there’s no gap for cold air to sneak in. The single layer of fabric across the front also retains heat better than a zip-up, which has two thinner panels sewn around a zipper track. For extreme cold, a heavyweight pullover with a high collar is the warmest option.

Can I wear a hoodie to work?

It depends on your workplace culture. In a casual office, a clean, solid-color hoodie without logos (especially a zip-up in a neutral tone) can look put-together when paired with chinos or dark jeans. Avoid overly baggy, distressed, or graphic hoodies for anything beyond extremely casual environments.

What is a French terry hoodie?

French terry is a knit fabric with smooth loops on the outside and soft, uncut loops on the inside. It’s lighter and more breathable than brushed fleece, making it a great choice for mild weather or layering. French terry hoodies drape more fluidly and are less bulky than traditional sweatshirt fleece.

Should I size up for a hoodie?

Only if you want a deliberately oversized, streetwear-inspired look or plan to layer heavily underneath. For a standard fit, stick with your regular size. Note that 100% cotton hoodies often shrink, so if you’re between sizes, sizing up is safer for length preservation.

What’s the difference between a hoodie and a sweatshirt?

The key difference is the hood. A sweatshirt is a crewneck pullover without a hood. A hoodie is a sweatshirt with a hood and often a kangaroo pocket. In casual usage, “hoodie” implies the hood; “sweatshirt” typically refers to the hoodless version.

Can I machine dry a hoodie?

You can, but you risk shrinkage and pilling. If you must use a dryer, set it to low heat and remove the hoodie while it’s still slightly damp, then lay it flat to finish drying. Air drying is far kinder to the fabric and preserves the fit longer.

In shortNo matter which style you choose, pay attention to fabric weight and fit first, then color and brand preferences. A well-made hoodie that suits your climate and daily routine can easily become your most-worn piece of clothing. Take care of it with gentle washing, and it’ll repay the effort for years.
