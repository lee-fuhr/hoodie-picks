How to choose a hoodies

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best hoodie for you depends on your climate, intended use, and preferred fit. Focus on fabric weight (lightweight for layering, midweight for everyday, heavyweight for warmth), construction quality (double-stitched seams, ribbed cuffs), and whether you want a zip-up or pullover. Reputable brands like Patagonia, Carhartt, and Champion offer reliable options across different price bands.

What fabric weight should you look for in a hoodie?

Hoodies generally fall into three weight categories: lightweight, midweight, and heavyweight. Lightweight hoodies, often made from French terry or thin cotton blends, excel as layering pieces under a jacket or for mild weather. Midweight hoodies use brushed fleece or thicker cotton and are the most versatile choice for everyday wear. Heavyweight hoodies (sometimes called “workwear” or “bush” hoodies) use dense, rugged fleece or loopwheel cotton and provide serious warmth and durability, but they can feel stiff until broken in. Pay attention to the grams per square meter (GSM) range: lighter hoodies typically fall in the 200–300 GSM range, while heavyweight ones can exceed 400 GSM. Unless you live in a very warm climate, a midweight hoodie with a brushed interior offers the best balance of comfort, breathability, and insulation. Check owner reviews for mentions of shrinkage, as some cotton-heavy hoodies can shrink significantly after washing.

Zip-up or pullover: which is better for your needs?

Zip-up hoodies offer unmatched versatility: you can wear them open like a jacket, zip up fully for warmth, or remove them entirely without pulling them over your head. They’re also easier to layer over other shirts or under a coat. The trade-off is that the zipper can fail over time (look for a sturdy, branded zipper) and the front often allows more heat to escape than a pullover’s solid panel. Pullover hoodies retain heat better because there’s no zipper gap, and they tend to have a cleaner, more traditional silhouette. They also avoid the “Y-front” look that some people dislike. However, you’ll have to pull them on and off, which can be a hassle if you’re wearing glasses or a hat. For all-day wear in cold conditions, many people prefer the warmth and simplicity of a pullover; for commuters and active use, zip-ups are often the better choice.

How should a hoodie fit?

Fit is highly personal, but a good baseline is a hoodie that skims your torso without being tight or baggy. The shoulder seam should sit at the edge of your shoulder, and the hem should hit just below your waistband, unless you’re deliberately going for a cropped or oversized look. Sleeve length should reach the base of your thumb when your arms hang naturally. Many brands now offer tailored (slim) and relaxed (standard) cuts. Slim-fit hoodies look cleaner under a jacket but can restrict movement if the fabric isn’t stretchy. Oversized hoodies are trendy and comfortable for lounging, but they can feel heavy and may not layer well. If you’re between sizes, consider what you’ll wear underneath: a t-shirt only vs. a button-down or flannel. Check the brand’s size chart and owner reviews for ‘true to size’ or ‘size up’ feedback before buying.

What features actually matter?

The hood itself is a key detail: a well-structured hood with a three-panel design stands up better and won’t collapse flat. Adjustable drawstrings (ideally flat cotton or metal-tipped cord) let you cinch the hood against wind. Avoid thin, slippery drawstrings that untie easily. Kangaroo pockets on pullovers should be deep enough to hold a phone or wallet without items falling out when you sit down. Some hoodies include a smaller media pocket inside or a zippered pocket for security. Ribbed cuffs and hem are essential for holding warmth and maintaining shape. Look for thick, dense ribbing that won’t stretch out after a few washes. For zip-ups, check that the zipper is YKK or an equivalent heavy-duty brand. Double-needle stitching on seams and reinforced stress points (like the hood attachment and pocket corners) are signs of long-lasting construction. A higher-quality hoodie often has flatlock seams or taped necklines that reduce chafing.

Which brands offer the best value for different budgets?

For budget-friendly options, brands like Hanes, Gildan, and Champion (their standard Reverse Weave line) provide solid basics that hold up reasonably well for the cost. These are great for throw-on wear, but thread count and seam quality may be lower. Mid-range brands such as Carhartt (the K87 and Rain Defender lines), Patagonia (their regular fleece hoodies), and Nike (Club Fleece) balance durability, comfort, and style. Carhartt’s workwear hoodies, for instance, are known for thick fabric and reinforced stitching. Premium options from American Giant, Reigning Champ, and Lady White Co. offer meticulous construction, specialized fabrics (like loopwheel cotton or terry blends), and refined fits that justify the higher price. These brands often have made-in-USA or made-in-Canada production, better shrink resistance, and more thoughtful detailing. If you can only own one hoodie and plan to wear it daily, stepping up to a mid-range or premium price band is usually worth it for longevity and comfort.

How do you care for a hoodie to make it last?

Most hoodies shrink and fade because of hot water and high heat drying. Turn your hoodie inside out before washing to protect the exterior print or fleece. Use cold water on a gentle cycle, and skip fabric softener, it coats the cotton fibers and reduces breathability. For heavy cotton hoodies, consider washing with a small amount of mild detergent and adding a half-cup of white vinegar to the rinse cycle to help set colors and soften the fabric naturally. Drying is the most critical step: lay the hoodie flat to air dry or tumble dry on low heat. Never wring out a wet hoodie; roll it in a towel to absorb excess moisture instead. For hoodies with a fuzzy interior, brushing the inside with a fabric comb or soft bristle brush after washing can restore some softness. Store hoodies folded rather than hung to prevent stretching the shoulders and hem.

Frequently asked questions

Are heavyweight hoodies always better quality?

Not necessarily. Heavier fabric can indicate denser construction and more warmth, but quality also depends on stitching, fiber length (long-staple cotton), and finishing. A midweight hoodie with tight knitting and reinforced seams may outlast a sloppily made heavyweight one.

Can I wear a hoodie for exercise?

Yes, but choose a lightweight or midweight hoodie in a moisture-wicking fabric like polyester or a cotton-poly blend. Pure cotton hoodies hold sweat and become heavy and cold when damp, making them less practical for high-intensity activity.

What’s the difference between a French terry and fleece hoodie?

French terry has loops on one side and a smooth exterior, making it lighter and more breathable, ideal for spring or layering. Fleece is brushed on both sides or the interior, trapping more air for warmth and a softer handfeel, but it can pill more easily.

Should I size up if I want an oversized fit?

Usually, yes, but check the brand’s specific measurements for chest and length. Some hoodies are already cut generously; sizing up once is typically enough to achieve a relaxed, oversized look without the sleeves becoming comically long.

Are zip-up hoodies less warm than pullovers?

On average, yes, because the zipper and front opening allow more heat to escape. Some zip-ups mitigate this with a wind flap behind the zipper, but for maximum insulation, a pullover is generally the warmer design.

How do I remove pilling from a hoodie?

Use a fabric shaver or a sweater comb to gently remove pills. For light pilling, running the hoodie inside out through a gentle wash cycle and then air drying can help, but the most effective method is a dedicated fabric shaver with a guard to avoid snagging the yarn.

In shortA great hoodie is an investment in everyday comfort. Prioritize the fabric weight and fit that matches your lifestyle, and don’t overlook the smaller details like pocket placement and hood structure. With proper care, a well-made hoodie can be your go-to layer for years.
